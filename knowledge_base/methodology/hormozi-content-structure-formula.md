# Hormozi Content Structure Formula

**Core insight:** If you're stuck on what to make content about, follow this 4-part structure. It works for any format (posts, newsletters, videos, podcasts).

## The Formula

### 1. One Main Idea / Lesson

Pick a single clear takeaway. Not three. Not a topic area. One specific, actionable insight.

**Example:** Adding physical products to intangible services dramatically increases conversions and profits.

### 2. Story / Example (1 deep or 2-3 short ones)

Stories make the idea real and believable. Use your own experience or client results. Specifics > generalities.

**Story Pattern:**
- **Context:** Who + what they were doing
- **Problem:** What wasn't working + why
- **Action:** What they changed (tied to your main idea)
- **Result:** Specific, quantified outcome

**Example — Story 1 (full):**
A gym owner in Gym Launch wanted to go online. Charging $2,000-$3,000 like everyone else. Wanted to get to $10,000. Added all the home equipment required for his program. Average ticket went from $2-3K to $10-15K. Equipment margin was significantly more than his services margin. Business scaled.

**Example — Story 2 (shorter):**
ACQ Vantage member taught ladies to print and sell stickers. Running five-day challenges selling $27/mo community. Couldn't afford ads. Added the sticker printing machine + yearly access for $500-$1,000. 15-30X price increase, likely no significant conversion drop + continuity revenue.

### 3. As Many REASONS as You Can List

**Critical distinction: Reasons, not steps.**

Steps = sequential instructions ("do this, then this").
Reasons = independent arguments for WHY the idea works.

Each reason is a standalone persuasion unit. The more reasons, the more airtight the argument. Readers who don't buy reason A might buy reason D.

**Example reasons (for bundling physical + intangible):**

A) **Comparison difficulty** — When people can't see exact costs, pricing comparison is harder → you can price premiumly.

B) **All-in-one > partial** — Complete solutions sell better than components.

C) **Margin surprise** — Physical products cost significantly less than consumers think, leaving massive margin when paired with services.

D) **Buying familiarity** — People are used to buying physical stuff. Intangibles feel riskier. Physical products lower the psychological barrier and increase conversion at higher price points.

E) **Demonstration ease** — Physical products explain themselves visually. A car sells in 5 minutes on a lot. Try selling a $50K intangible that fast. Same principle applies to knives, solar roofs, Amazon products.

F) **Competitive moat** — Adding physical products shows investment and expense, making it harder for competitors to copy your business model.

### 4. Soft CTA

Not a hard sell. A gentle nudge placed at the end, often in a P.S. or after a "ta da" transition moment.

**Example:** "PS: We opened a new workshop date. It's two days in Vegas. My team will give you the 3-5 next steps to take in your business. Book a call here to see if it's a fit."

## Why This Formula Works (Meta-Analysis)

This structure aligns with and extends the Content Unit Framework:

| Formula Part | Content Unit Equivalent | Why It Works |
|---|---|---|
| One Idea | **Hook** — clear value promise | Single idea = easy to hook with specificity |
| Stories | **Retain** — narrative retention | Stories keep attention via "what happens next?" |
| Reasons | **Retain + Reward** — stacked value | Each reason is a mini-reward. More reasons = more value per second |
| Soft CTA | **Ask** — intermittent/integrated | Soft CTA preserves the give:ask ratio |

### Reasons vs. Steps: When to Use Which

| Use Reasons When | Use Steps When |
|---|---|
| Persuading someone to adopt an idea | Teaching someone HOW to do something |
| The audience needs to be convinced | The audience is already convinced |
| Each argument is independent | Order matters for execution |
| Content goal = belief shift | Content goal = skill transfer |

**Key insight:** Most content creators default to steps. Reasons are underused but often more engaging because each reason is a standalone "aha moment" rather than part of a sequence you must follow entirely to get value.

## Business Insight: Physical + Intangible Bundling

The content example itself contains a powerful pricing/offer insight worth noting:

**Pattern:** Service/info businesses hit a price ceiling because intangibles are hard to compare and easy to undervalue. Adding a physical product:
- Creates price anchoring confusion (can't comparison-shop easily)
- Signals investment/legitimacy
- Leverages consumers' existing comfort with buying physical goods
- Provides margin that often exceeds the original service margin

**Application to GTM:** When clients struggle with pricing power or conversion on intangible offers, consider physical product bundling as a strategic lever — not just a content topic.
