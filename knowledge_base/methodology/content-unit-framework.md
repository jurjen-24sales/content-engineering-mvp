# Content Unit Framework

A content unit is the smallest amount of material it takes to hook, retain, and reward attention. It can be as little as an image, a meme, or a sentence. All three can happen simultaneously (a viral tweet does all three at once). Longer content is simply multiple content units linked together. A new comedian gets a few minutes (one content unit); a master comic gets an hour (many linked units).

## The Three Components

### 1. Hook: Get Them to Notice

**Objective:** Give them a reason to redirect attention from whatever they're doing toward you. Effectiveness = percentage of people who start consuming your content.

Three levers to hook attention:

#### Topics (What you make content about)

Use personal experiences - there's only one of you. Five categories:

| Category | What It Is | Example |
|----------|-----------|---------|
| **Far Past** | Important life lessons connected to your product/service | "A friend found 3 hours of daily screen time when I said I had 'no time'" |
| **Recent Past** | Stories from recent meetings, conversations, interactions | A meeting with a CEO copying competitors led to a tweet about differentiation |
| **Present** | Ideas captured in real-time, noted publicly | Tweet ideas as they happen; if one performs well, make more on that topic |
| **Trending** | Apply your expertise to what's trending now | Relevant commentary on current events that touches your domain |
| **Manufactured** | Create an experience, then make content about it | "I lived on $100 for a month. Here's how." |

#### Headlines (Why they should consume it)

Based on meta-analysis of news headlines. Include at least two:

| Component | Description | Example |
|-----------|------------|---------|
| **Recency** | As recent as possible | Something from an hour ago > a year ago |
| **Relevancy** | Personally meaningful to audience | Nurses care about nurse stuff, not accountant stuff |
| **Celebrity** | Prominent people | We wouldn't care about a random breakfast, but Jeff Bezos's breakfast? Yes. |
| **Proximity** | Close to home geographically | Neighbor's house on fire > house across the country |
| **Conflict** | Opposing ideas, people, forces | Pineapple on pizza? Freedom vs Security? |
| **Unusual** | Odd, unique, rare, bizarre | Anything outside the norm grabs attention |
| **Ongoing** | Stories still in progress | Person in labor = updates every 10 minutes |

#### Format (Make it look like what they expect)

Match the popular format of the platform. People consume content similar to stuff they've liked before. Format your content for the platform first, then tweak for your ideal audience. Use the best content targeting your market as your guide.

### 2. Retain: Get Them to Keep Consuming

**Driver:** Curiosity. Embed unresolved questions in their minds. Three methods:

| Method | What It Is | Example |
|--------|-----------|---------|
| **Lists** | Things, facts, tips presented one after another following a theme | "7 Ways I Invested $1000 in My 20s That Paid Off Big" |
| **Steps** | Actions in a specific order that accomplish a goal | "3 Steps to Creating a Great Hook" |
| **Stories** | Events (real or imaginary) with a lesson or takeaway | "My editor made me do 19 drafts of this book - here's what I did to him" |

**Key difference:** Steps are ordered and have an explicit reward (accomplish the goal). Lists are flexible and have a less explicit reward. Stories drive curiosity because people want to know what happens next.

**Interweaving:** You can have lists within steps, stories about each list item, a list of stories, ongoing storylines. The creativity is unlimited. This chapter itself has lists within steps and stories interweaving them.

### 3. Reward: Satisfy the Reason They Consumed

**The real question:** Is it good? Does it satisfy the reason they watched? Does it make them want to share?

**Quality metric:** How often it rewards the audience in the time they spend consuming it. Think value per second.

> "There is no such thing as too long, only too boring."

**How to know if you succeeded:** Your audience grows. If it's not growing, your stuff isn't that good. The audience decides, not you.

**Reward fails when:**
- You promise 7 ways and deliver 4
- You deliver 7 ways but they're all stale or heard before
- You're talking to the wrong audience (spouse tips to single people)
- You promise marketing strategies for dentists but they can't use them

**Bottom line:** Clearly satisfy the hook you used to get attention. Completely answer the unresolved questions you embedded.

## Short vs. Long Form Content

Shorter content hooks, retains, and rewards fewer times. Longer content does it more times. Doing it more times takes more skill because you have to string more "good" content units in a row.

Start small, build from there. Many successful authors started with short stories before writing novels.
